\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

How does a population of anonymous agents, starting from a perfectly symmetric 50-50 behavioural split and no shared history, arrive at a self-enforcing social norm---not merely a behavioural convention, but a shared rule that agents internalise, comply with, and enforce \citep{bicchieri2006grammar}?

Formal models of convention emergence---most prominently \citet{young1993evolution}---show that bounded-memory learning drives populations toward behavioural regularities. But the resulting conventions are coordination equilibria sustained by best-response incentives: agents conform because conforming pays, not because they hold an internalised prescriptive rule. Cognitive theories of norms \citep{conte1995cognitive} argue that genuine norms require mental representations---internal states that \emph{encode} the rule, distinct from the behavioural tendency to follow it---yet do not specify the micro-level mechanism by which repeated social exposure crystallises into such a representation. The missing piece is a cognitive account of how observing a descriptive regularity becomes holding a prescriptive internal state---the passage from \emph{descriptive} to \emph{social} norm in \citeauthor{bicchieri2006grammar}'s (\citeyear{bicchieri2006grammar}) terms.

This paper proposes a dual-memory cognitive architecture in which each agent maintains two qualitatively distinct memory systems---experiential and normative---unified through a single bridging variable: predictive confidence. We show that the interaction between these systems produces a \emph{normative cascade}: a self-amplifying process that drives the population from disorder to consensus in time largely independent of population size (verified across $N = 20$ to $20{,}000$; scaling exponent $\alpha \approx 0.03$). Through systematic ablation, tick-by-tick mechanism tracing, Granger causal analysis, and formal mediation decomposition, we establish that the cascade requires the cooperation of both memory systems.

The first mechanism---\emph{experiential memory}---draws on the decisions-from-experience paradigm. \citet{hertwig2010decisions} showed that human decision-makers rely on small, recent samples rather than exhaustive histories; computational models that best predict human behaviour in repeated games use effective windows of five to six trials \citep{nevo2012bi}. Crucially, this window is not fixed. \citet{behrens2007learning} demonstrated that the brain continuously tracks environmental volatility and adjusts its learning rate---in volatile environments, learning rate increases (equivalent to shortening the memory window); in stable environments, it decreases. We operationalise these findings as a confidence-adaptive first-in-first-out (FIFO) buffer: predictive confidence~$C$ serves as the agent's estimate of environmental stability (the inverse of perceived volatility), and the effective window length $w$ is a monotonically increasing function of~$C$. Successful predictions raise~$C$ and expand the window; failures lower~$C$ and contract it. The update is asymmetric---confidence builds additively but collapses multiplicatively---so that agents respond quickly to environmental change, consistent with the faster learning rates that \citeauthor{behrens2007learning} observed under high volatility.

The second mechanism---\emph{normative memory}---operationalises norm internalisation as a drift-diffusion process. \citet{germar2014social} demonstrated experimentally that exposure to majority opinion alters the drift rate in perceptual decision-making, shifting evidence accumulation toward the majority-consistent response. \citet{germar2019learning} further showed that this perceptual shift persists after the social influence is removed---evidence that repeated exposure can produce a lasting cognitive change, not merely transient compliance. We model this as a signed two-boundary drift-diffusion model (DDM) in which the agent's experiential belief signal accumulates as confidence-gated evidence; when accumulated evidence crosses a crystallisation threshold, a discrete normative rule forms. This threshold plays a role structurally analogous to \citeauthor{granovetter1978threshold}'s (\citeyear{granovetter1978threshold}) adoption threshold, but operates at the individual cognitive level through internal evidence integration rather than as a population-level response function.

The key architectural decision---that uncertainty promotes norm uptake while certainty promotes reliance on personal experience---follows from a well-established principle in social learning: organisms increase reliance on social information when individual information is unreliable. \citet{rendell2010copy} showed that ``copy when uncertain'' strategies dominate in competitive tournaments; \citeauthor{boyd1985culture}'s (\citeyear{boyd1985culture}) conformist transmission framework provides a theoretical account of why such strategies are adaptive. In our architecture this principle is implemented through confidence-gating: the DDM drift rate scales with $(1-C)$, so agents whose predictions have been failing accumulate normative evidence rapidly, while confident agents rely on experiential memory.

Predictive confidence~$C$ thus bridges the two memory systems, governing whether learning is dominated by individually accumulated experience or by socially inferred rules.

This paper makes three main contributions:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=4pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Novel cognitive architecture.} We introduce a dual-memory architecture---experiential and normative---grounded in established cognitive mechanisms, that produces norm emergence endogenously from purely local interactions without any global coordination signal.
\item \textbf{Normative cascade identification.} We identify and characterise a self-amplifying cascade (scaling exponent $\alpha \approx 0.03$), providing causal decomposition via Granger causality tests, early warning signal detection, and formal mediation analysis.
\item \textbf{Necessity proof via ablation.} We demonstrate that neither memory system alone suffices across population sizes up to $N = 20{,}000$; the normative speedup grows from $3\times$ at $N = 20$ to $76\times$ at $N = 20{,}000$, establishing that the cooperation of both systems is essential.
\end{enumerate}

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:model} specifies the model and frames the theoretical scope of the regularities it can produce. Section~\ref{sec:results} presents a systematic ablation isolating each mechanism's contribution, then traces the \emph{normative cascade}---the positive-feedback process by which minority norms dissolve and the population locks into consensus---through tick-by-tick analysis, Granger causality tests, and early warning signal detection. Section~\ref{sec:boundaries} establishes the cascade's scaling properties across four orders of magnitude in~$N$ and examines how enforcement pressure reshapes the dynamics and where it breaks down. Section~\ref{sec:robustness} reports robustness checks across seeds and strategy symmetry. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} positions the results within the norm-emergence literature and discusses limitations.

\section{Model Description}
\label{sec:model}
%% ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Environment and task}

$N$ agents (even) repeatedly play a symmetric pure coordination game with strategy set $S = \{A, B\}$. There is no payoff asymmetry---$A$ and $B$ are \emph{a priori} equivalent. Each tick proceeds through a synchronous six-stage pipeline.

\paragraph{Information structure.} At each tick, agents are randomly paired via uniform permutation. Each agent observes only its partner's chosen strategy (and optionally $V$ additional strategies sampled from other pairs). No agent ever observes any global statistic. All information is local.

\subsection{Agent state}

Each agent carries two memory systems, a confidence tracker, and derived action variables:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
\toprule
Component & Variables & Role \\
\midrule
Experiential memory & FIFO buffer, $b_{\exp}$, $w$ & Track partner action frequencies \\
Confidence & $C \in [0,1]$ & Prediction accuracy tracker \\
Normative memory & $r$, $\sigma$, $a$, $e$ & Norm rule, strength, anomaly, DDM evidence \\
Derived & compliance $= \sigma^k$, $b_{\mathrm{eff}}$ & Action probability \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\subsection{Per-tick pipeline}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Stage 1: Pair and act.} Random permutation forms $N/2$ pairs. Each agent computes an effective belief $b_{\mathrm{eff}}$ and samples an action via probability matching ($P(\text{play } A) = b_{\mathrm{eff}}^A$). Each agent also makes a MAP prediction of its partner's action.

\item \textbf{Stage 2: Observe and update experiential memory.} The partner's action is pushed into a FIFO circular buffer (capacity $= w_{\max}$). The experiential belief is recomputed from the most recent $w$ entries:
\begin{equation}
b_{\exp}^A = \frac{\#A \text{ in last } w \text{ entries}}{w}
\label{eq:bexp}
\end{equation}
Additionally, $V$ strategies are sampled from other pairs for the normative observation set $O_i(t)$.

\item \textbf{Stage 3: Confidence update.} The agent compares its MAP prediction to the partner's actual action:
\begin{equation}
C \leftarrow \begin{cases}
C + \alpha(1 - C) & \text{if prediction correct (additive increase)} \\
C(1 - \beta) & \text{if prediction wrong (multiplicative decay)}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:confidence}
\end{equation}
The memory window is then recomputed: $w = w_{\mathrm{base}} + \lfloor C \cdot (w_{\max} - w_{\mathrm{base}}) \rfloor$. High confidence $\to$ long memory; low confidence $\to$ short memory.

\item \textbf{Stage 4: Normative update.} This stage branches based on crystallisation state.

\item \textbf{Stage 5: Enforcement.} Crystallised agents above a strength threshold ($\sigma > \theta_{\mathrm{enforce}}$) send a signal to partners who violated their norm. The signal enters the partner's DDM as a one-tick-delayed push: $\Phi (1-C) \gamma_{\mathrm{signal}} \cdot \mathrm{dir}$. (Analysis in Section~\ref{sec:enforcement}.)

\item \textbf{Stage 6: Metrics.} Read-only computation of population-level statistics.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Experiential memory: mean-field estimation}
\label{sec:exp}

Since partners are drawn by random pairing, each observation is an independent sample from the population's action distribution. The FIFO buffer stores $w \in [w_{\mathrm{base}}, w_{\max}]$ such samples, so $b_{\exp}^A$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:bexp}) is an unbiased but noisy estimator of the true population frequency $f_A$:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[b_{\exp}^A] = f_A, \qquad \mathrm{Var}(b_{\exp}^A) = \frac{f_A(1 - f_A)}{w}
\end{equation}

With $w \in [2, 6]$, this variance is substantial. Convergence under experiential memory alone is a \emph{diffusion} process: each agent independently drifts toward the majority, one noisy sample at a time.

\subsection{Normative memory: the DDM-crystallisation system}
\label{sec:norm}

Normative memory operates in two distinct modes, forming a state machine:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{@{}c@{}}
\texttt{NO NORM} $\xrightarrow{|e| \geq \theta_{\mathrm{crystal}}}$ \texttt{HAS NORM}
$\xrightarrow[\sigma < \sigma_{\min}]{\text{crisis} \to \text{dissolve}}$
\texttt{NO NORM}
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\paragraph{Pre-crystallisation (DDM).} An evidence accumulator $e$ integrates the experiential belief signal:
\begin{equation}
e \leftarrow e + \underbrace{(1 - C) \cdot f_{\mathrm{diff}}}_{\text{confidence-gated drift}} + \underbrace{s_{\mathrm{push}}}_{\text{enforcement signal}}
\label{eq:ddm}
\end{equation}
where $f_{\mathrm{diff}} = b_{\exp}^A - b_{\exp}^B$ is the signed difference in the agent's experiential belief (DD-11). The DDM uses the FIFO-smoothed belief rather than raw single-tick observations; this provides first-level temporal smoothing, while the accumulator provides second-level integration. No additive Gaussian noise is included (DD-12): sampling noise is already captured by $b_{\exp}$'s finite-window variance; adding $\sigma_{\mathrm{noise}}$ on top would double-count uncertainty and re-introduce noise-driven crystallisation at 50-50. When $|e| \geq \theta_{\mathrm{crystal}}$, the agent \emph{crystallises} a norm: $r = A$ if $e > 0$, $r = B$ if $e < 0$. The norm strength is initialised to $\sigma_0$.

Key property: the drift is gated by $(1 - C)$. Low-confidence agents (uncertain about the environment) accumulate evidence faster and crystallise sooner.

At a symmetric 50-50 population ($f_{\mathrm{diff}} \approx 0$ because $b_{\exp} \approx (0.5, 0.5)$), the drift is near zero and \emph{crystallisation does not occur}. The accumulator oscillates around zero, waiting for $b_{\exp}$ to drift away from 0.5 through experiential learning. When the population is asymmetric (e.g., 70\% $A$), $b_{\exp}$ shifts toward $A$, producing a positive drift that drives crystallisation predominantly toward the majority direction.

\paragraph{Post-crystallisation (norm maintenance).} Once crystallised, the agent monitors conformity and violations among its observations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Conformity} (observed action matches norm $r$): $\sigma$ increases via $\sigma \leftarrow \sigma + \alpha_\sigma(1 - \sigma)$.
\item \textbf{Violation} (observed action $\neq r$): anomaly counter $a$ increments.
\item \textbf{Crisis}: when $a \geq \theta_{\mathrm{crisis}}$, the norm weakens: $\sigma \leftarrow \lambda_{\mathrm{crisis}} \cdot \sigma$, and $a$ resets to 0.
\item \textbf{Dissolution}: if $\sigma < \sigma_{\min}$ after a crisis, the norm is abandoned. The agent returns to the DDM ($e = 0$) and may crystallise a new norm---potentially for the opposite strategy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Effective belief: the blending equation}
\label{sec:blend}

A crystallised agent's action probability blends the norm direction with the experiential estimate:
\begin{equation}
b_{\mathrm{eff}}^A = \underbrace{\sigma^k}_{\text{compliance}} \cdot b_{\mathrm{norm}}^A + (1 - \sigma^k) \cdot b_{\exp}^A
\label{eq:beff}
\end{equation}
where $b_{\mathrm{norm}}^A = 1$ if $r = A$ and $0$ if $r = B$, and $k$ is a compliance exponent. For an $A$-crystallised agent with $\sigma = 0.8$, $k = 2$: compliance $= 0.64$, so $b_{\mathrm{eff}}^A = 0.64 + 0.36 \cdot b_{\exp}^A$. Even if $b_{\exp}^A = 0.5$, the agent acts $A$ with probability $0.82$.

An uncrystallised agent simply uses $b_{\mathrm{eff}} = b_{\exp}$ (compliance $= 0$).

\subsection{Initialisation}

All agents start identical: $b_{\exp} = (0.5, 0.5)$, $C = C_0$, $r = \text{none}$, $\sigma = 0$, $e = 0$. The population is perfectly symmetric with respect to $A$ and $B$.

\subsection{What kind of norm can this architecture produce?}
\label{sec:norm_status}

Before turning to results, it is worth framing the theoretical scope of the model.  \citeauthor{bicchieri2006grammar}'s (\citeyear{bicchieri2006grammar}) taxonomy distinguishes a behavioural convention---a regularity sustained by mutual best-response \citep{lewis1969convention}---from a social norm, which additionally requires \emph{normative expectations} (beliefs about what others think one \emph{ought} to do) and conditional compliance.  The architecture above can produce regularities that go beyond a Lewis convention in four respects: (i)~the crystallised state $(r, \sigma)$ is an explicit internal representation distinct from the experiential belief~$b_{\exp}$; (ii)~crystallised agents enforce; (iii)~norms are robust to transient perturbations in $b_{\exp}$ (decay requires the anomaly--crisis pathway); (iv)~dissolution and recrystallisation create an irreversible population-level ratchet despite individual-level reversibility.

Two features, however, fall short of Bicchieri's full definition: agents hold no second-order beliefs and compliance is not conditional on normative expectations.  The emergent regularity thus occupies a middle ground---more than a convention, less than a full social norm.  We refer to it as the \emph{functional substrate} of normativity: the minimal cognitive infrastructure from which genuine social norms could emerge if second-order beliefs and conditional compliance were added.  Crucially, neither addition would disable the cascade mechanism (which operates through anomaly accumulation, not metacognition), so the results below characterise a robust micro--macro bridge that should survive enrichment of the cognitive model.

\section{Results: Ablation and Cascade Mechanism}
\label{sec:results}
\label{sec:ablation}

\subsection{Experimental design}

To isolate each memory system's contribution, we ran a $3 \times 2$ factorial:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Factor 1---Experiential memory level} (3 levels):
  \begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \emph{None}: $b_{\exp}$ reset to 0.5 after every tick (no individual learning).
    \item \emph{Fixed}: FIFO learning with fixed window $w = 5$.
    \item \emph{Dynamic}: FIFO learning with confidence-driven window $w \in [2, 6]$.
  \end{itemize}
\item \textbf{Factor 2---Normative memory}: OFF / ON.
\end{itemize}

Crossed with three population sizes $N \in \{20, 100, 500\}$. Each cell was run for 100 independent trials ($T = 3000$ ticks max).

\paragraph{Convergence criterion.} The full model defines convergence as three layers simultaneously met for 50 consecutive ticks: (i)~behavioural majority $\geq 0.95$; (ii)~belief error $< 0.10$ and belief variance $< 0.05$; (iii)~fraction crystallised $\geq 0.80$ and fraction holding dominant norm $\geq 0.90$. The \emph{convergence tick} reported throughout is the first tick at which this sustained criterion begins (i.e.\ the start of the 50-tick window). For ablation conditions where normative memory is OFF, only the behavioural layer is applicable; we therefore report convergence based on the behavioural majority $\geq 0.95$ criterion sustained for 50 ticks.

\subsection{Observations}

Three patterns stand out from Tables~\ref{tab:ablation}--\ref{tab:speedup}.

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Experiential memory is necessary; normative memory is not sufficient.} Without experiential learning (None rows), $b_{\exp}$ is pinned at $0.5$, so $f_{\mathrm{diff}} = 0$ and the DDM evidence accumulator never reaches threshold---crystallisation cannot occur.\footnote{The modest majority increase from $\sim$0.53 (None+OFF) to $\sim$0.59 (None+ON) arises from within-tick transient signals: Stage~2 computes $b_{\exp}$ before the post-tick reset, giving the DDM a brief non-zero drift that enables some symmetric crystallisation, which cannot drive convergence.}

\item \textbf{Experiential learning alone scales poorly; adding norms changes the scaling class.} Without norms, convergence tick grows roughly linearly with~$N$ and Fixed-window convergence degrades to 24\% at $N{=}500$. With norms, convergence is 100\% everywhere and the tick barely increases (25→43 over a 25-fold $N$ range). The growing speedup ratio (Table~\ref{tab:speedup}) is not a constant factor---it signals a qualitative mechanism change, analysed further at large~$N$ in Section~\ref{sec:scaling}.

\item \textbf{Normative memory compensates for weak experiential learning.} Fixed+Norm and Dynamic+Norm converge at nearly identical speeds. Once the cascade fires, the quality of the experiential estimator barely matters.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{The normative cascade: tick-by-tick mechanism}
\label{sec:cascade}

The ablation shows \emph{what} each system contributes; we now trace \emph{how}. Every agent's internal state was tracked tick by tick in a single diagnostic trial ($N = 100$, Dynamic+Norm, seed = 42).

    \label{fig:anatomy}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Symmetric random} (tick 1--6). All agents uncrystallised. Actions are coin flips based on $b_{\exp} \approx 0.5$. DDM evidence $e$ remains near zero because $f_{\mathrm{diff}} = b_{\exp}^A - b_{\exp}^B \approx 0$.

\item \textbf{Experiential drift and asymmetric crystallisation} (tick 6--30). Stochastic fluctuations in random pairing cause $b_{\exp}$ to drift from 0.5 for individual agents. As $b_{\exp}$ shifts, $f_{\mathrm{diff}}$ becomes non-zero and DDM evidence begins accumulating. Agents whose $b_{\exp}$ drifts furthest---and whose confidence $C$ is lowest---crystallise first. Because crystallisation requires a \emph{sustained} directional signal from $b_{\exp}$ (not noise), the earliest crystallisers are biased toward the emerging stochastic majority. By tick 30, the split is already asymmetric: 38\,$A$ vs.\ 23\,$B$, with 39 agents still uncrystallised. This built-in asymmetry---absent in a noise-driven model---means the cascade starts from a stronger directional signal.

\item \textbf{Tipping} (tick 30--37). Crystallised agents' behavioural commitment (via $b_{\mathrm{eff}}$, Eq.~\ref{eq:beff}) amplifies the majority signal in the environment. Experiential memory shifts $b_{\exp}$ rapidly toward $A$. First $B$-norm dissolutions appear at tick 37 as $B$-norms accumulate anomalies faster than $A$-norms.

\item \textbf{Cascade} (tick 37--55). The $B$-norm count collapses from 23 to 0 in $\sim$18 ticks. Each dissolution feeds the next (see Section~\ref{sec:loop}). By tick 55, all remaining agents are $A$-crystallised.

\item \textbf{Lock-in} (tick 55+). All agents are $A$-crystallised. Norm strength recovers, confidence rises to 1.0, $b_{\exp} \to 1.0$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{The one-way ratchet}
\label{sec:ratchet}

We tracked every norm-state transition for all 100 agents. Among agents that crystallised to $B$, dissolved, and subsequently re-crystallised:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item 20 agents followed $B \to \text{dissolved} \to A$.
\item \textbf{0 agents} followed $B \to \text{dissolved} \to B$.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{The dual feedback loop}
\label{sec:loop}

The cascade arises from two interlocking positive-feedback loops.

\paragraph{Loop 1: Differential erosion (slow, tick 30--37).} When $n_A > n_B$ among crystallised agents, a $B$-norm agent's partner is more likely to play $A$ (a norm violation). Therefore $B$-norms accumulate anomalies faster, reach crisis sooner, and experience more $\sigma$-decay. As $B$-agents' compliance drops (Eq.~\ref{eq:beff}), they act less distinctly $B$, which makes the environment even more $A$-biased:
\begin{equation}
n_A > n_B \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{anomaly rate}(B) > \text{anomaly rate}(A) \;\Longrightarrow\; \sigma_B \downarrow\; \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{environment more } A\text{-biased}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Loop 2: Dissolution cascade (fast, tick 37--55).} When $\sigma_B$ falls below $\sigma_{\min}$, the agent dissolves and re-enters the DDM. In the now strongly $A$-biased environment, the one-way ratchet (Section~\ref{sec:ratchet}) ensures recrystallisation to~$A$ within ${\sim}10$ ticks. Each conversion increases $n_A$, accelerating both loops:
\begin{equation}
\frac{d\, n_B}{dt} \;\propto\; -\,n_B \;\cdot\; p_A(n_B), \qquad \frac{\partial\, p_A}{\partial\, n_B} < 0
\end{equation}
(This is a qualitative continuous approximation; the actual dynamics proceed through discrete crisis events---anomaly accumulation to $\theta_{\mathrm{crisis}}$, multiplicative $\sigma$-decay, and dissolution when $\sigma < \sigma_{\min}$.)

\noindent The result is \emph{exponential acceleration}: each dissolution makes the next faster (Figure~\ref{fig:causal}d). This is the structural reason why the cascade completes in time independent of~$N$: unlike experiential-only convergence---where each agent independently drifts at rate $\sim 1/w$, giving $O(N)$ scaling---the anomaly--crisis--dissolution pathway operates locally at every minority-norm agent simultaneously, so the cascade time depends on the crisis parameters, not on population size. The speedup ratio in Table~\ref{tab:speedup} grows with~$N$ for exactly this reason.

\end{figure}

\noindent Section~\ref{sec:scaling} confirms this parallelism quantitatively: dissolution counts scale as $O(N)$ while cascade completion time remains effectively $O(1)$ across a 200-fold range in population size.

\subsection{Temporal causal verification}
\label{sec:granger}

The dual feedback loop described above (Section~\ref{sec:loop}) posits a specific temporal causal order: anomaly accumulation drives dissolution, which shifts the belief field, which accelerates further anomaly accumulation. We verify this order using Granger causality tests on the tick-level population time series from the same diagnostic trial (seed~$= 42$, $N = 100$, $T = 300$; the dynamics in the first 200 ticks are identical to the run described above, with additional post-lock-in observations).

Granger causality tests whether past values of variable~$X$ improve prediction of~$Y$ beyond~$Y$'s own history---a necessary (though not sufficient) condition for $X \to Y$ causation \citep{granger1969investigating}. We fit vector autoregressive models at lags~1--3 and test the null of zero coefficients on the $X$-lags via an $F$-test; Table~\ref{tab:granger} reports lag-1 results for cross-pair comparability (results at AIC-optimal lags---which range from 1 to 4 across pairs---are qualitatively identical). Because the cascade is a phase transition, several series are non-stationary; augmented Dickey--Fuller tests failed to reject the unit-root null at the 5\% level for $\bar{C}$, $\bar{b}_{\mathrm{eff}}$, and $\bar{\sigma}$ (norm strength). These three series were first-differenced before testing; the remaining series (anomaly count, belief shift) are stationary in levels.

Three findings merit attention:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{The anomaly $\to$ dissolution link is extremely strong} ($F = 138.1$). \item \textbf{The $\sigma \to b_{\mathrm{eff}}^A$ feedback loop is confirmed} ($F = 10.9$, $p = 0.001$). Changes in norm strength Granger-cause changes in the population's effective belief---precisely the amplification mechanism described in Section~\ref{sec:loop}: as minority norms weaken, their holders' $b_{\mathrm{eff}}^A$ shifts toward the majority, further biasing the environment.

\item \textbf{Confidence does not drive norm strength} ($F \approx 0$, $p = 1.0$). Although confidence~$C$ gates the DDM drift and modulates compliance, changes in confidence have no temporal predictive power over norm strength dynamics. The cascade operates through the anomaly--crisis--dissolution pathway, not through confidence-mediated gradual erosion. This does not mean confidence is unimportant---it shapes \emph{which} agents crystallise first (Section~\ref{sec:norm})---but once norms exist, their fate is determined by the anomaly dynamics, not by confidence trajectories.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Scale invariance of causal structure.} To verify that these causal relationships are not artefacts of small-$N$ dynamics, we repeated the Granger tests at $N \in \{1{,}000,\; 5{,}000,\; 10{,}000,\; 20{,}000\}$ using independent diagnostic trials (seed $= 42$). The anomaly~$\to$~$\sigma$ link remains significant at lag~2 across all $N$ tested ($F = 3.7$--$10.0$, $p < 0.05$), confirming that the causal chain identified at $N = 100$ is a genuine scale-invariant property of the cascade mechanism, not a small-$N$ sampling artifact.

\subsection{Early warning signals}

\label{sec:ews}

Self-amplifying cascades in complex systems are often preceded by \emph{early warning signals}---statistical signatures that appear before the transition itself \citep{scheffer2009early}. A common precursor is increasing variance: as the system approaches a tipping point, fluctuations grow because the restoring forces are weakening \citep{dakos2012methods}.

We applied CUSUM (Cumulative Sum) changepoint detection \citep{page1954continuous} to the same tick-level time series. The detector maintains two running sums---one for upward shifts, one for downward---updating at each tick as $S^+_t = \max(0,\, S^+_{t-1} + z_t - k)$, where $z_t = (y_t - \hat\mu)/\hat\sigma$ is normalised against a baseline estimated from the first 20~ticks, and $k = 0.5$ is a slack parameter. A changepoint is declared when $S^+_t$ (or $S^-_t$) exceeds the decision threshold $h = 4.0$. Three changepoints were detected:

\centering

\label{tab:changepoints}

\toprule
Series & Event & Tick \\
\midrule

Mean norm strength ($\bar{\sigma}$) & Norm strength collapse & 84 \\
Crystallised agent count & Crystallisation cascade & 86 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The temporal ordering is significant: the belief shift precedes the crystallisation cascade by \textbf{19~ticks}. This lead time constitutes an early warning window---the population's experiential beliefs are already shifting toward the majority direction well before the normative cascade becomes visible in crystallisation counts.

Note that the CUSUM-detected ticks (67, 84, 86) are later than the visually identified phase boundaries in Section~\ref{sec:cascade} (cascade at tick~37--55). This is expected: CUSUM is a \emph{cumulative} detector that declares a changepoint only after sufficient statistical evidence has accumulated against the baseline, introducing a detection lag. The underlying dynamics are identical (same seed), but the CUSUM alarm fires after the deviation has been sustained long enough to exceed the decision threshold $h$. The relevant finding is not the absolute tick numbers but the \emph{relative ordering}---belief shift is detected before crystallisation count change---and the 19-tick lead time between the two CUSUM alarms.

To test for increasing pre-cascade variance, we computed the rolling variance (window $= 20$~ticks) of $\bar{b}_{\mathrm{eff}}^A$ in the pre-changepoint period (ticks~1--67). The Pearson correlation between rolling variance and time is $r = 0.877$---a strongly increasing trend. As the system approaches the cascade tipping point, belief fluctuations grow in amplitude because the stabilising forces (symmetric population composition) are weakening. The pattern is consistent with the early warning signatures described by \citet{scheffer2009early} for tipping points in complex systems, though we note that our discrete-agent system does not satisfy the formal dynamical-systems prerequisites (smooth potential landscape, slow--fast variable separation) of the critical slowing down framework; the analogy is statistical rather than structural.

The 19-tick early warning window suggests that, in principle, an observer monitoring belief variance could anticipate the cascade before it occurs---a prediction that could be tested experimentally in human-subject coordination games.

\subsection{Enforcement pressure}

The scaling analysis above established the cascade at $\Phi = 0$. We now ask two questions: does $\Phi$ shift the phase transition boundary (known at $\theta \approx 7$ for $\Phi = 0$), and does $\Phi$ create qualitatively different convergence pathways? (A fine-grained $\Phi$ sweep probing non-monotonicity is reported in Appendix~\ref{app:nonmonotonic}.)

\paragraph{Method.}
All experiments use the full-model configuration ($N = 100$, $w_{\mathrm{base}} = 2$, $w_{\max} = 6$, $V = 0$, normative enabled) with default crystallisation parameters ($\sigma_0 = 0.8$, $k = 2$, $\theta_{\mathrm{crisis}} = 10$). Only $\theta_{\mathrm{crystal}}$ and $\Phi$ are varied. Each condition was run for 30 independent trials ($T = 3000$). The phase boundary and pathway analyses use independently drawn trial batches; because each batch contains only 30 trials, modest variation in convergence rates for nominally identical conditions is expected. Beyond standard convergence metrics, we tracked six novel per-trial pathway metrics via a manual tick loop with transition detection:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Total dissolutions}---number of norm dissolution events across all ticks.
\item \textbf{Total enforcements}---cumulative enforcement signals sent.
\item \textbf{Recrystallisation direction}---among dissolved agents that re-crystallised, how many switched norm (\emph{flip}) versus returned to the same norm (\emph{same}).
\item \textbf{Peak norm split}---$\max_t [\max(n_A, n_B) / (n_A + n_B)]$ among crystallised agents (worst polarisation).
\item \textbf{Time in churn}---ticks where both dissolutions and new crystallisations occurred simultaneously.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Interpretation: two faces of enforcement}
\label{sec:two_faces}

The results reveal that enforcement operates through two competing channels:

\paragraph{Beneficial channel: DDM acceleration.} Enforcement signals deliver a push $\Phi(1-C)\gamma_{\mathrm{signal}} \cdot \mathrm{dir}$ into pre-crystallised agents' evidence accumulators. When the enforcing agent holds the majority norm, this accelerates crystallisation in the majority direction. This channel dominates when many agents remain pre-crystallised---i.e., when $\theta_{\mathrm{crystal}}$ is high.

\paragraph{Harmful channel: mutual destabilisation.} When two agents hold opposing crystallised norms, enforcement triggers mutual violations (DD-7: enforcement against a post-crystallised partner is wasted, and the violation counts as anomaly). This accelerates crisis on both sides, increasing dissolution and potentially creating churn cycles. This channel dominates when most agents are already crystallised---i.e., when $\theta_{\mathrm{crystal}}$ is low and agents crystallise early into a roughly symmetric split.

\paragraph{The balance.} The relative strength of these channels depends on the fraction of agents that are pre-crystallised when enforcement begins. At $\theta = 7$, the DDM barrier is high enough that the enforcement-beneficial window is large---many agents are still accumulating evidence when the first crystallised agents begin enforcing. At $\theta = 5$, the barrier is low enough that by the time enforcement becomes active (requiring $\sigma > \theta_{\mathrm{enforce}} = 0.7$), most agents have already crystallised to both sides, and enforcement primarily creates cross-norm destabilisation.

This mechanistic account also explains the high dissolution rate at $\theta = 3$ even at $\Phi = 0$ (12.4 dissolutions): with a very low barrier, agents crystallise as soon as $b_{\exp}$ drifts even slightly from 0.5, before any strong behavioural asymmetry develops. This ensures roughly equal numbers crystallise on each side. The subsequent cascade must dissolve the entire minority side---a process that enforcement amplifies but cannot disrupt, because the one-way ratchet is maintained by the experiential memory signal that grows stronger as the cascade progresses.

\subsubsection{Quantitative mediation test}
\label{sec:mediation}

The two-channel interpretation above (Section~\ref{sec:two_faces}) implies a specific causal chain: $\Phi$ affects convergence speed \emph{through} enforcement-driven dissolutions, not (or not only) through a direct effect. We tested this formally using Baron--Kenny mediation analysis with bootstrap confidence intervals on the 243 converged runs from the pathway dataset.

\paragraph{Path 1: $\Phi \to$ enforcements $\to$ convergence speed.}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Mediation path coefficients (outcome: $\log t_{\mathrm{conv}}$).}
\label{tab:mediation}
\begin{tabular}{@{}llrl@{}}
\toprule
Path & Coef.\ & $p$ & Interpretation \\
\midrule
$c$ (total: $\Phi \to \log t_{\mathrm{conv}}$) & $+0.012$ & --- & Small positive total effect \\
$a$ ($\Phi \to$ enforcements) & $+68.6$ & ${<}\,0.001$ & $\Phi$ strongly increases enforcement count \\
$b$ (enforcements $\to \log t_{\mathrm{conv}}$ $\mid$ $\Phi$) & $+0.0002$ & ${<}\,0.001$ & More enforcement $\to$ slower convergence \\
$c'$ (direct: $\Phi \to \log t_{\mathrm{conv}}$ $\mid$ enforcements) & $-0.004$ & n.s.\ & Direct effect reverses sign \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

\noindent Indirect effect $a \times b = 0.016$ (Sobel $z = 4.70$, $p < 0.001$; bootstrap 95\% CI $[0.010, 0.024]$, $B = 2000$). The proportion mediated is 1.33---exceeding 1.0, which indicates a \emph{suppression effect}: the direct effect of $\Phi$ is actually \emph{negative} ($c' = -0.004$), meaning enforcement accelerates convergence through a non-mediated channel (DDM acceleration of pre-crystallised agents), but this beneficial effect is masked and reversed by the positive indirect pathway through enforcement-induced turbulence.

\paragraph{Path 2: $\Phi \to$ dissolutions $\to$ convergence speed.}

\noindent Indirect effect $= 0.012$ (Sobel $z = 3.90$, $p < 0.001$; bootstrap 95\% CI $[0.005, 0.020]$). Proportion mediated $= 1.01$. Both bootstrap confidence intervals exclude zero.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The mediation maps directly onto the two channels identified in Section~\ref{sec:two_faces}: the beneficial channel (DDM acceleration) appears as the negative direct effect $c' < 0$; the harmful channel (mutual destabilisation) appears as the positive indirect effect $a \times b > 0$. The suppression pattern (proportion mediated $> 1$) is the statistical signature of two opposing causal pathways.

\subsection{Symmetry between strategies}

Of the 30 seeds, 16 converged to $A$ and 14 to $B$---consistent with the model's complete symmetry. The winning side is determined by stochastic fluctuations during the symmetry-breaking phase.

\subsection{Small-population regime}
\label{sec:small_N}

To test whether the normative speedup vanishes when populations are small enough for experiential memory alone to suffice, we ran the Dynamic condition (Norm ON vs.\ OFF, $w_{\mathrm{base}}{=}2$, $w_{\max}{=}6$) at $N \in \{4, 6, 8, 10, 16, 20\}$ with 100 trials per cell and $T_{\max}{=}3{,}000$. All conditions achieved 100\% convergence at every $N$, confirming that small populations are easy for both memory systems.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/fig_small_N_speedup.png}
    \caption{Normative speedup ratio (mean convergence tick without normative memory divided by mean convergence tick with normative memory) as a function of population size $N$. Error bars show $\pm 1$ SE via the delta method. The dashed line marks ratio${=}1$ (no speedup).}
    \label{fig:small_N_speedup}
\end{figure}

The speedup is never unity: even at $N{=}4$ the normative system provides a $1.5\times$ advantage, because the cascade mechanism still accelerates consensus once crystallisation begins. As $N$ grows, the speedup rises monotonically---from $1.5\times$ ($N{=}4$) through $2.6\times$ ($N{=}10$) to $3.2\times$ ($N{=}20$)---because each agent's experiential window ($w \leq 6$) covers a shrinking fraction of the population, degrading the experiential signal-to-noise ratio while the cascade remains a local, parallel process. Combined with the large-$N$ results in Table~\ref{tab:large_N_speedup}, the full speedup curve spans $N{=}4$ to $N{=}20{,}000$: normative memory is always beneficial, and its advantage grows from $1.5\times$ to $76\times$ across nearly four orders of magnitude in population size.

Three points of contrast merit brief comment.  First, our experiential-memory-only condition (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) reproduces essentially Young's dynamic---diffusive, $O(N)$, degrading at large~$N$---confirming that the normative layer, not a modelling trick, drives the scaling change.  Second, unlike Granovetter/Watts cascades, adoption here is reversible at the individual level yet irreversible at the population level via the one-way ratchet (Section~\ref{sec:ratchet}); this self-correcting mechanism has no analog in standard threshold models.  Third, our cascade resembles \citeauthor{centola2007complex}'s (\citeyear{centola2007complex}) complex contagion (requiring sustained evidence, not single exposure), yet propagates efficiently on a well-mixed network because each agent accumulates evidence independently from random pairings---the cascade is inherently parallel rather than sequential.

The DDM-crystallisation mechanism is directly grounded in \citeauthor{germar2014social}'s (\citeyear{germar2014social}; \citeyear{germar2019learning}) experimental findings that majority exposure shifts perceptual drift rates and that this shift persists after removal of the social signal.  The ``copy when uncertain'' principle \citep{rendell2010copy,boyd1985culture} maps onto our confidence-gating ($\text{drift} \propto 1 - C$); the DDM formulation adds temporal specificity, predicting \emph{when} crystallisation occurs and \emph{whether} it can be reversed.

\subsection{Testable predictions}
\label{sec:predictions}

The model generates four predictions testable in laboratory coordination games:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Confidence and norm adoption.}  Agents with lower predictive confidence crystallise earlier (Eq.~\ref{eq:ddm}: drift $\propto 1 - C$).  In a multi-round coordination game with tracked prediction accuracy, subjects whose recent accuracy is low should be faster to adopt a behavioural rule---and harder to dislodge once they have.  This extends \citeauthor{germar2014social}'s (\citeyear{germar2014social}) paradigm from perceptual to strategic settings.

\item \textbf{Early warning signals.}  Population-level belief variance should increase before a norm cascade becomes visible in adoption counts (Section~\ref{sec:ews}).  Real-time monitoring of stated beliefs or action probabilities in a lab setting should reveal a monotonically increasing variance trend in the pre-cascade window, with a lead time proportional to the cascade magnitude.

\item \textbf{The one-way ratchet.}  If a nascent norm is experimentally disrupted (e.g., by temporarily removing committed subjects), the disrupted subjects should re-adopt the majority norm upon return, not revert to the minority position.  The model predicts zero same-direction recrystallisation after dissolution in a majority-$A$ environment.

\item \textbf{Scaling independence.}  In a condition that allows norm expression (e.g., subjects can declare and enforce a rule), convergence time should be approximately independent of group size.  In a minimal-information control (observation only, no norm expression), convergence should slow linearly with group size.  The predicted ratio between conditions should grow with~$N$, from ${\sim}1.5\times$ at $N{=}4$ (Figure~\ref{fig:small_N_speedup}) to ${\sim}76\times$ at $N{=}20{,}000$ (Table~\ref{tab:large_N_speedup}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations and extensions}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Well-mixed population.}  Random pairing assumes a complete interaction graph.  Real social networks are structured: clustered neighbourhoods, high-degree hubs, community boundaries.  Network topology could qualitatively alter the cascade---clustering might sustain persistent minority-norm pockets that resist dissolution \citep{centola2007complex}, while hubs might seed cascades disproportionately.  Whether the $O(1)$ scaling survives topological constraint is the most pressing open question.

\paragraph{Symmetric coordination game.}  $A$ and $B$ are payoff-equivalent by construction.  This is a useful theoretical control---the norm is purely endogenous---but real norms often coordinate on payoff-asymmetric equilibria.  Extending to asymmetric games would test whether the cascade can lock in Pareto-dominated norms, a question central to the classical norm-emergence literature.

\paragraph{No communication.}  Agents observe actions but cannot signal intentions.  Human norm emergence routinely involves explicit discussion, argumentation, and persuasion.  Adding even a simple signalling channel could enable second-order beliefs (``my partner signalled~$A$, so they probably hold an $A$-norm''), potentially bridging the gap between our proto-norms and Bicchieri's full social norms.

\paragraph{Homogeneous agents.}  All agents are identical at initialisation.  Heterogeneity in $\theta_{\mathrm{crystal}}$ or $\theta_{\mathrm{crisis}}$ would produce stratified adoption: some agents as norm entrepreneurs (low threshold, fast crystallisation), others as late adopters.  This connects to \citeauthor{granovetter1978threshold}'s (\citeyear{granovetter1978threshold}) insight that the \emph{distribution} of thresholds, not just the mean, determines cascade dynamics.

\paragraph{Calibration.}  Several normative-layer parameters ($\theta_{\mathrm{crystal}}$, $\theta_{\mathrm{crisis}}$, $\sigma_{\min}$, $\lambda_{\mathrm{crisis}}$) are set by calibration rather than derived from empirical data.  The phase boundary analysis (Section~\ref{sec:boundary}) shows that the cascade mechanism is robust across $\theta_{\mathrm{crystal}} \in [3, 5]$, but quantitative predictions (cascade duration ${\sim}35$ ticks) depend on these choices.  Connecting these parameters to experimentally measured quantities---for instance, mapping $\theta_{\mathrm{crystal}}$ to the number of consistent social exposures needed to shift a subject's drift rate in a Germar-type paradigm---would ground the model empirically and narrow the free-parameter space.

\paragraph{Binary strategies.}  The model uses a two-strategy coordination game.  Extension to multi-strategy settings would test whether the cascade mechanism can select among multiple competing norms---a question of both theoretical and practical interest for understanding real-world norm diversity.

\section{Summary}
\label{sec:summary}
%% ════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
\item \textbf{Neither memory system alone is sufficient.} Experiential memory provides a noisy mean-field sensor that can drive convergence, but with $O(N)$ scaling that degrades at large populations (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}). Normative memory amplifies weak experiential signals into strong behavioural commitments, but without an experiential signal to amplify, convergence cannot occur.

\item \textbf{Together they produce a self-amplifying cascade.} The dissolution--recrystallisation cycle creates two interlocking feedback loops whose completion time is effectively independent of~$N$ ($\alpha \approx 0.03$; Tables~\ref{tab:large_N_sweep},~\ref{tab:cascade_scaling}). The normative speedup grows with population size (Table~\ref{tab:large_N_speedup}), and above $N{=}10{,}000$ normative memory becomes a prerequisite for reliable convergence.

\item \textbf{The cascade has a verifiable causal structure.} Granger causality confirms the anomaly~$\to$~dissolution~$\to$~belief-shift chain at every $N$ tested (Section~\ref{sec:granger}). Belief variance increases monotonically before the cascade, providing a potential early warning window (Section~\ref{sec:ews}).

\item \textbf{Enforcement has two opposing channels} whose balance depends on the crystallisation threshold (Section~\ref{sec:two_faces}). Formal mediation analysis confirms a suppression effect: the direct channel accelerates convergence while the indirect channel, through enforcement-driven turbulence, slows it (Section~\ref{sec:mediation}).

\item \textbf{All of this emerges from purely local interactions}---no agent ever observes the global state.
\end{enumerate}
